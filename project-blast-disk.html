Overview

Why a blast disk matters

In high-power rocketry, recovery is the most safety-critical phase of flight. At apogee — the highest point of a rocket's trajectory — a separation event must fire to deploy the parachute before descent. Fail this, and the rocket becomes an uncontrolled projectile.

A blast disk is a precisely engineered frangible disk that ruptures at a specific pressure when the ejection charge fires. Too strong, and the charge can't break it. Too weak, and it ruptures prematurely from aerodynamic pressure during ascent. The design window is unforgiving — and that's exactly what makes it interesting.

Duke AERO's vehicles target 10,000+ ft AGL, meaning the disk must also account for varying ambient pressure at altitude, thermal cycling from cold soaks during ascent, and propellant gas chemistry. This isn't a bolt-on component — it's a systems-level problem.

Core Design Challenge
Engineer a separation disk with a consistent, predictable failure mode — not a random fracture, but a clean, repeatable burst — while surviving the structural loads of supersonic flight up to apogee.
Design Process

How we got there

01
System Requirements & Constraints
I started by working with the avionics and recovery subteams to establish hard requirements: ejection charge pressure output range, maximum ascent dynamic pressure, allowable weight budget, and material compatibility with hot propellant gases. This cross-team alignment was critical — the blast disk exists at the intersection of three subsystems.
02
Material Selection & Geometry Ideation
I evaluated candidate materials — 6061 aluminum, 7075 aluminum, and brass — against burst pressure, corrosion resistance to black powder residue, and machinability. I then sketched geometric concepts for the score pattern: concentric ring scores, cross scores, and radial petal cuts, each creating different failure modes and burst pressure profiles.
03
FEA Analysis in ANSYS
I modeled each geometry variant in SolidWorks and imported into ANSYS Mechanical for static structural and fatigue analysis. By varying score depth and width across parametric studies, I mapped the burst pressure as a function of geometry — targeting a burst pressure of [X] psi ± 3%. I paid particular attention to stress concentration at score roots, which dictated final score radius.
04
Prototype Fabrication
Prototypes were machined on a manual lathe and CNC mill in the Duke student machine shop. The score geometry required careful tooling selection — a carbide V-cutter at consistent feed rate — to achieve repeatable score dimensions. Three iterations refined the score depth and surface finish based on bench testing results.
05
Burst Pressure Testing
We built a pneumatic test fixture to incrementally pressurize mounted disks with N₂ gas, logging burst pressure for each sample. This gave us a statistical distribution of failure pressures, validating our FEA predictions and establishing manufacturing tolerances. Results shaped the final score geometry specification.

Technical Depth

Engineering specifics

Score geometry: The final design uses a radial petal score pattern — 4 symmetric cuts radiating from a central relief hole — chosen over concentric rings because it produces a clean, predictable petal-fold failure mode that clears the body tube without fragmenting. Each score is [depth] mm ± 0.05 mm, machined with a 60° V-cutter.

Material: 6061-T6 aluminum was selected over brass for its combination of consistent yield strength (276 MPa), excellent machinability, and resistance to corrosion from black powder residue. The T6 temper provides a well-characterized stress-strain curve that makes FEA predictions reliable.

FEA methodology: Static structural analysis with a refined mesh at score roots (element size 0.1 mm) using von Mises failure criterion. Burst pressure was defined as the pressure at which average stress across the minimum cross-section reaches 0.9× UTS — validated against burst test data within 5%.

Key Insight
Early prototypes failed inconsistently — sometimes tearing instead of folding. Root cause analysis revealed surface roughness at the score root acting as a stress riser. Specifying Ra ≤ 1.6 µm finish at the score resolved the issue and reduced burst pressure variance by ~40%.

Reflection

Lessons & takeaways

🔗
Systems Thinking First
The blast disk cannot be designed in isolation. Every dimension is a negotiation with the ejection charge pressure, the body tube bore, and the avionics timing. I learned to always start with interface requirements before touching any geometry.
📊
FEA Is a Tool, Not an Answer
FEA gave me a directional prediction, but the burst test data revealed manufacturing variability that simulation couldn't capture. The combination of simulation and physical testing produced a far more robust design than either alone.
🔧
Tolerance Chains Matter
A 0.1 mm variation in score depth produced a measurable shift in burst pressure. Writing GD&T specs that machinists could actually achieve — not aspirational tolerances — was just as important as the design itself.
🗣️
Cross-Team Communication
I was surprised how much time was spent aligning with avionics and propulsion. Clear interface control documents (ICDs) early in the design process would have saved several rounds of revision. I'm building that habit now.
