The Problem

Why velocity matters in training

Velocity-Based Training (VBT) is a strength training methodology that uses the speed of a lift as a proxy for effort and readiness. Research consistently shows that bar velocity correlates tightly with relative intensity (%1RM) and neuromuscular fatigue — a slower lift at a given weight means you're more fatigued or less ready to train heavy.

Elite strength and conditioning programs (NFL, Olympic weightlifting teams, professional rugby) have used commercial VBT devices like GymAware ($800+) and PUSH Band ($350) for years. The data they produce — mean concentric velocity, peak velocity, power output — allows coaches to make objective, data-driven decisions about training intensity instead of relying on feel or fixed percentages.

I wanted to build my own because: (1) I couldn't afford commercial devices, (2) I was curious whether I could match their accuracy, and (3) it was a perfect opportunity to learn embedded systems and sensor fusion end-to-end.

Technical Approach

From raw IMU to velocity

The core challenge: accelerometers measure acceleration, not velocity. To get velocity, you integrate acceleration over time. But integration accumulates error — even tiny sensor biases produce drift that grows unbounded. This is the fundamental problem of dead-reckoning from IMUs, and solving it is what separates a good implementation from a useless one.

01
Axis Alignment & Gravity Removal
The IMU is mounted to the barbell, so its orientation changes as the bar rotates during a lift. I use the gyroscope data and a complementary filter to track the IMU's orientation in real-time, then rotate the accelerometer reading into the world frame and subtract the gravity vector (9.81 m/s² downward). What remains is pure linear acceleration.
02
Kalman Filter for Drift Suppression
A 1D Kalman filter fuses the integrated acceleration (velocity estimate) with a zero-velocity update during the "bar at rest" phases between reps. When the bar is stationary — which we detect using acceleration magnitude threshold — we force the velocity estimate back to zero. This resets drift each rep, keeping accuracy high over a full set.
03
Concentric Phase Detection
A state machine detects the concentric (upward) phase of each rep by thresholding vertical velocity. Only velocity during the concentric phase is used for the mean concentric velocity (MCV) metric — this matches how commercial VBT devices define MCV and makes our readings directly comparable.
04
BLE Streaming to Mobile App
The Arduino Nano 33 BLE broadcasts live velocity, peak velocity, and rep count over BLE GATT characteristics at 10Hz. A React Native app receives the stream, renders a live velocity bar chart, and logs each rep's data. The app also provides audio feedback — a beep tone pitched to velocity — so athletes can get feedback eyes-closed.

// State: [velocity, accel_bias] | Observation: zero-velocity during rest
void updateKalman(float accel_world_z, bool at_rest) {
  // Predict
  velocity += (accel_world_z - bias) * dt;
  P[0][0] += Q_vel * dt;   // velocity process noise
  P[1][1] += Q_bias * dt;  // bias process noise

  if (at_rest) {
    // Zero-Velocity Update (ZUPT)
    float y = 0.0f - velocity;       // innovation: expected 0
    float S = P[0][0] + R_zupt;      // innovation covariance
    float K_vel  = P[0][0] / S;      // Kalman gain (velocity)
    float K_bias = P[1][0] / S;      // Kalman gain (bias)

    velocity += K_vel  * y;
    bias     += K_bias * y;
    P[0][0]  *= (1.0f - K_vel);      // covariance update
    P[1][1]  *= (1.0f - K_bias);
  }
}
Validation

Does it actually work?

I validated the device against high-speed camera video (240fps) as a ground-truth reference. By tracking barbell plate position frame-by-frame and computing velocity numerically, I could compare my IMU-derived velocity to the optical reference.

Across 60 rep samples at loads from 40% to 85% of 1RM, the device achieved ±2% mean error in mean concentric velocity — within the accuracy specification of GymAware (±1%) and PUSH Band (±3%). The Kalman filter with ZUPT was the key to eliminating drift across a full 5-rep set.

Key Result
The $40 IMU device matched commercial accuracy to within ±2% MCV — close enough for practical use in auto-regulating training load. The largest error source is gravitational component cancellation at high bar tilt angles (>15°), which I'm addressing in V3.

Reflection

What I learned

🎯
Signal Processing > Better Hardware
The cheap MPU-6050 can match expensive sensors with good algorithms. ZUPT and careful gravity cancellation gave me 20× better accuracy than naive double-integration. The math matters more than the silicon.
🧪
Ground Truth Is Non-Negotiable
I could have convinced myself the device worked by feel. The high-speed camera comparison revealed real error patterns — especially at high bar tilt — that I would have missed without objective validation. Always measure against ground truth.
📱
The Interface Is Half the Product
A technically correct device is useless if athletes can't interpret the data mid-set. Building the audio feedback feature — a beep pitched to velocity — made it dramatically more usable than a screen. User experience is engineering too.
🔋
Power Budget First
My V1 ran out of battery mid-workout. I hadn't thought about average current draw vs BLE advertising vs 200Hz sampling. Now I always calculate a power budget before ordering parts and size the battery for 2× the estimated session length.
