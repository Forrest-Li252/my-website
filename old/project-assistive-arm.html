The Human Need

Engineering for a specific person

Spastic cerebral palsy causes involuntary muscle contractions that limit range of motion and make everyday tasks — reaching for a glass, pointing at something, waving — exhausting or impossible. For children, this limits participation in play, school, and social life during critical developmental years.

Existing assistive arm devices are expensive, bulky, and designed for adults. They ignore children's smaller anthropometry, higher activity levels, and the reality that a child will refuse to wear something that looks "medical." Our challenge was to design something that actually gets used — which meant centering the child's experience, not just their biomechanics.

"He won't wear the brace from the clinic. He says it makes him feel different from his friends. If it looks like a toy or a superhero thing, maybe he'd try it."
— Parent, needs assessment interview

Design Process

How the design evolved

01
Needs Assessment & Stakeholder Interviews
I conducted 6 semi-structured interviews with the child, parents, an occupational therapist, and a pediatric physiatrist. Rather than arriving with design concepts, I used open-ended questions to understand the child's daily routines, the tasks most limited by spasticity, and what they wished they could do. The OT provided critical insight into safe ranges of motion and contraindicated positions. This phase fundamentally shaped the design — the comfort and aesthetic requirements were just as non-negotiable as the mechanical ones.
02
Biomechanics Modeling
I modeled the arm as a two-segment linkage (upper arm + forearm) with distributed mass loads. I computed gravity torques at the shoulder and elbow across the full range of motion — these are the loads the device must counteract to achieve gravity compensation. I then validated my model against published pediatric anthropometric data for the child's age group, scaling for their specific arm length measurements.
03
Spring Selection & Mechanism Design
The gravity compensation mechanism uses a constant-force spring at the shoulder joint — selected for its relatively flat force-deflection curve, which provides consistent support across the full ROM rather than peak support at one angle. I calculated the required spring constant from the gravity torque model and sourced off-the-shelf springs that matched, avoiding custom spring costs.
04
Prototype Iterations
Four prototypes refined the design across structure, fit, and usability. Each iteration was tested with the child and OT, with structured feedback sessions that produced specific design changes. Iteration was the most valuable part of the process — no amount of modeling predicted that the harness straps would irritate the child's skin or that the original arm trough was too rigid for comfortable all-day wear.
V1
Proof of Concept — Gravity Compensation Validated
3D-printed PLA frame with a torsional spring at the shoulder. Tested the biomechanics model in practice — spring force was sufficient. Major issues: 640g (too heavy), straps uncomfortable, trough fit poor.
Reduce weight & redesign harness interface
V2
Lightweight Frame — PETG + Foam Padding
Switched to PETG with lattice-fill structure to reduce weight to 420g. Added foam padding to trough. Child wore for 20 min with no discomfort complaints. Still too stiff in forearm trough — limited pronation/supination.
Add forearm rotation DOF
V3
Forearm DOF + Adjustability
Added a passive rotational joint in the forearm segment. Added sliding adjustment for arm length to accommodate growth. 380g. Child wore for full 1-hour OT session. Feedback: "It doesn't feel heavy" — positive response to aesthetics.
Refine spring attachment, optimize harness
V4
Final Design — 310g, Full Adjustability
Final structural optimization, padded neoprene harness, aluminum spring bracket replacing plastic. 310g. OT measured +40° shoulder flexion ROM vs unassisted. Child asked to take it home.
Mission achieved — child initiated use independently

Technical Depth

Engineering specifics

Gravity compensation mechanism: The shoulder joint uses a constant-force spring (k = [X] N/mm) pretensioned to balance the gravitational torque at mid-ROM. Unlike a simple torsional spring, a constant-force spring maintains approximately equal compensation across the full arc — critical because the child needs assistance in all positions, not just one.

Arm kinematics: The forearm segment rotates passively around a pin joint, providing pronation/supination freedom while maintaining gravity compensation from the upper arm spring. This two-DOF passive mechanism allows natural reaching motions without fighting the device.

Structural analysis: FEA in SolidWorks confirmed that critical load cases — fall arrest (10g impact load at hand), maximum spring force — produce stresses well below the yield strength of PETG at relevant print orientations. The aluminum spring bracket was introduced when FEA showed plastic was marginal at the spring attachment boss.

Most Impactful Design Decision
Switching from a rigid arm trough to a semirigid neoprene sleeve in V3 was the single change that made the child willing to wear the device for extended periods. It looks less clinical, conforms to the arm, and allows natural skin breathing. The best engineering decision wasn't structural — it was material choice driven by user experience.

Reflection

What I learned

👂
Listening Is a Design Skill
The most important things I learned about this project came from conversations, not calculations. The child's offhand comment about looking "different" reshaped our aesthetic direction. Listening — really listening, not just gathering requirements — is a core engineering competency I didn't expect to develop here.
🔄
Iterate with the User, Not for Them
Every prototype was tested with the child present. We didn't evaluate prototypes in isolation and then present a final design — we co-refined. Watching the child try to use each version revealed problems that no amount of expert review could have caught.
⚖️
Specs vs Preferences
The OT's clinical requirements (safe ROM limits, joint alignment) were non-negotiable. The child's aesthetic preferences were equally non-negotiable — without meeting both, the device doesn't get used. I learned to hold both sets of requirements with equal seriousness.
🌍
Real Impact Is Measurable
+40° ROM is meaningful to me because I know what specific activities it unlocked — the child can now reach the middle of the table during meals without assistance. Numbers in a report matter when you know the human story behind them.
